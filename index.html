Why factoring is hard

Every time you buy something online, send a private message, or log into your bank, your security depends on a single mathematical bet: that nobody can efficiently undo multiplication.

Multiplying two prime numbers is easy. Any computer can compute $61 \times 53 = 3{,}233$ in a fraction of a second. But going the other direction is surprisingly difficult. If I hand you the number $3{,}233$ and ask you to find its prime factors, you have to search. For this small number the search is quick. But for numbers with hundreds of digits, the kind used in real encryption, the search space is so vast that no known classical algorithm can finish before the sun burns out.

This asymmetry isn't some minor technicality. It's the foundation of RSA encryption, the system that protects most of the internet. The entire edifice of modern cryptography rests on the belief that factoring large numbers is effectively impossible.

In 1994, the mathematician Peter Shor showed that this belief has a crack.2 He discovered a quantum algorithm that factors numbers exponentially faster than any known classical method. What makes Shor's result so striking isn't brute force. The algorithm doesn't simply try factors faster. Instead, it transforms the factoring problem into something completely different: finding the period of a repeating mathematical function. And period-finding turns out to be something quantum computers do remarkably well.

The key idea: factoring reduces to period-finding

If we want a fast quantum factoring algorithm, we can't just "try all possible divisors in parallel." That's a common misconception about quantum computing. If you put all possible divisors into superposition and then measured, you'd just get a random divisor, which almost certainly wouldn't be the right one.

Instead, we need to exploit some structure in the factoring problem: a mathematical property that makes factoring different from just finding a needle in a haystack. Fortunately, factoring has a lot of structure. For one thing, every positive integer has exactly one prime factorization. By contrast, if someone handed you a Sudoku puzzle, you'd have no way of knowing whether it had one solution, a million solutions, or none at all. This uniqueness hints that factoring might have other nice mathematical properties. And it does.

The property Shor exploits is the connection between factoring and period-finding. Here's how it works. Suppose you want to factor some number $N$. Pick any random number $a$ that shares no common factor with $N$ (in mathematical language, $\gcd(a, N) = 1$). Now look at what happens when you compute successive powers of $a$, taking the remainder after dividing by $N$ each time:

$$a^0 \bmod N,\quad a^1 \bmod N,\quad a^2 \bmod N,\quad a^3 \bmod N,\quad \ldots$$

If you haven't seen modular arithmetic before: $a \bmod N$ simply means the remainder when you divide $a$ by $N$. So $49 \bmod 15 = 4$, because $49 \div 15 = 3$ with remainder $4$.

The remarkable fact, guaranteed by number theory, is that this sequence always repeats. There is some smallest positive integer $r$, called the period or order, such that $a^r \equiv 1 \pmod{N}$, and then the entire pattern starts over from the beginning.

Why does the period tell us anything about the factors? There's a beautiful result, discovered by Euler in the 1760s: if $N = p \times q$ for primes $p$ and $q$, then the period $r$ must evenly divide $(p-1)(q-1)$.1 So for $N = 15$ with $p = 3$ and $q = 5$, we get $(p-1)(q-1) = 8$, and indeed the period $r = 4$ divides $8$. The period is leaking information about the prime factors.

More concretely, once you know $r$ and it happens to be even, the factors of $N$ can usually be found by a simple computation:

$$\gcd\!\left(a^{r/2} - 1,\; N\right) \qquad \text{and} \qquad \gcd\!\left(a^{r/2} + 1,\; N\right)$$

Why does this work? Since $a^r \equiv 1 \pmod{N}$, we know that $N$ divides $a^r - 1$. If $r$ is even, we can write $a^r - 1 = (a^{r/2} - 1)(a^{r/2} + 1)$. So $N$ divides this product. If neither factor is itself divisible by $N$, then $N$ must split its prime factors between the two terms. And $\gcd$ extracts them.

An analogy may help. Imagine a clock with an unknown number of hours. You watch the hand move, and after exactly $r$ ticks, it returns to where it started. That period reveals the hidden structure of the clock. Shor's algorithm uses the period of $a^x \bmod N$ to reveal the hidden structure of $N$ in exactly this way.

Where the quantum computer comes in

At this point you might be wondering: we just found the period by computing a table. Why on earth do we need a quantum computer?

The answer is scale. For $N = 15$, the period is $4$, and we only needed to evaluate four values to find it. But for a number with 600 digits, the period could be comparably enormous. A classical computer would need to evaluate a staggering number of modular exponentiations, one after another, to find where the pattern repeats. No classical shortcut is known.

Here's the crucial subtlety, though. A quantum computer can't just "try all divisors in parallel" and magically pick the right one. That's a common misconception. If you put all possible values into superposition and then measured, you'd just get a random value, which is useless. What a quantum computer can do is detect a global property of the entire sequence: its period.1 The period isn't hiding in any single value. It's a pattern that only emerges from looking at all the values together. And that's exactly the kind of structure quantum interference can extract.

To understand how, we need to introduce a few ideas from quantum mechanics. Don't worry if these are new to you. We'll build up the intuition step by step.

A brief guide to quantum notation

In quantum computing, we use a notation called ket notation. When you see something like $|5\rangle$, it just means "the quantum state representing the number 5." The vertical bar and angle bracket are just a label. You can think of $|x\rangle$ as a box with the number $x$ written on it.

A classical computer stores a definite value: a register might hold the number 5. A quantum register can be in a superposition of many values at once. This is often written as $|0\rangle + |1\rangle + |2\rangle + \ldots$, meaning the register is simultaneously in all of those states. When you measure a register in superposition, you get one of those values at random, and the superposition collapses.

Two more ideas will be important. Interference is what happens when quantum states combine. Just like sound waves, quantum amplitudes can reinforce each other (constructive interference, making an outcome more likely) or cancel each other out (destructive interference, making it less likely). And entanglement is a correlation between two quantum registers: measuring one can instantly affect what you'd see if you measured the other.

With that vocabulary in hand, here's how the quantum part of Shor's algorithm works. Rather than computing $a^x \bmod N$ for each value of $x$ one at a time, the quantum computer places all possible values of $x$ into superposition and evaluates the function for all of them at once. Then it applies the Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT), which uses interference to extract the period from the pattern.

An analogy for the QFT

Scott Aaronson offers a wonderful intuition for what the QFT actually does.1 Imagine a wall covered in analog clocks. One clock makes a full revolution every 17 hours, another every 26 hours, another every 24.7 hours, and so on for every possible period you can imagine. Beneath each clock is a posterboard with a thumbtack pinned in the middle.

Now imagine someone keeping a strange schedule, waking up at irregular times. Every time they wake up, they walk along the wall and move each thumbtack one inch in whatever direction the corresponding clock's hand is pointing.

What happens to the thumbtacks? For most clocks, the directions are essentially random across many days, so the thumbtacks drift around but stay near the middle. The pushes cancel out. But for the clock whose period matches the person's actual schedule, the hand always points in the same direction at wake-up time. So that one thumbtack travels far from the center, in a straight line.

By looking at which thumbtack ends up farthest from the middle, you discover the person's hidden schedule. That is exactly what the QFT does. The "thumbtacks" are the quantum amplitudes; for the wrong period, contributions from different parts of the superposition point in different directions and cancel via destructive interference; for the right period, they all point the same way and add up constructively. Measurement then samples the loud peak.

The actual quantum circuit

Here's what the quantum circuit for Shor's algorithm looks like. The top wires are the counting register (the qubits that will store our measurement). The bottom wire is the work register, initialized to $|1\rangle$. Each box represents a quantum gate. Time flows left to right.

Quantum circuit for Shor's algorithm
A real quantum circuit for Shor's algorithm. The output $y$ approximates $k \cdot Q/r$ for a random $k$.

Reading the circuit left to right: each Hadamard gate (H) on the counting register puts a single qubit into a 50/50 superposition. Together they put the whole register into a uniform superposition over all $2^m$ values. Then the controlled-$U$ gates apply $a, a^2, a^4, \ldots, a^{2^{m-1}}$ to the work register, each controlled by a counting qubit. This is how the modular exponentiation gets done in superposition, using the repeated-squaring trick. Finally, the inverse QFT converts the periodicity into a measurable peak, and the measurement reads out an integer $y$ that we then post-process classically to find $r$.

This is the actual circuit IBM and Google run on their quantum hardware. For $N = 15$, it requires 12 qubits total (8 counting + 4 work). For larger numbers, both registers grow. A 2048-bit RSA key would need a few thousand logical qubits, but error correction inflates this to millions of physical qubits.

You might be wondering: doesn't computing $a^x \bmod N$ for enormous $x$ take forever? If $x$ were 300 quadrillion, would we need to multiply $a$ by itself 300 quadrillion times? Fortunately not. A technique called repeated squaring lets us compute $a^x \bmod N$ using only about $\log_2(x)$ multiplications. The idea is to break $x$ into powers of 2 (its binary representation) and compute $a^1, a^2, a^4, a^8, \ldots$ by squaring each time. This is exactly what those controlled-$U$ gates above are doing, one squared power at a time.

Deeper look: phase estimation and the multiplication operator

The previous section described what the quantum circuit does: superposition, modular exponentiation, QFT, measurement. But it may have felt like a recipe without an explanation. Why does the QFT extract the period? What's really going on?

This section goes one level deeper. If you're comfortable with the recipe and want to move on, feel free to skip ahead to the next section. But if you want to understand the mechanism, read on.

Consider the operation "multiply by $a$, modulo $N$." In quantum computing, we can build a gate that performs this operation on a quantum state. We write it as $M_a$, and it acts like this: if you feed it a state $|x\rangle$ (remember, that's just "the state representing the number $x$"), it outputs $|ax \bmod N\rangle$. In other words, it multiplies by $a$ and takes the remainder.

For $N = 15$ and $a = 2$, this gate maps $|1\rangle \to |2\rangle \to |4\rangle \to |8\rangle \to |1\rangle$, cycling with period $r = 4$. Notice it's doing the same modular exponentiation we computed by hand earlier, but now as a quantum operation.

Here's the key mathematical insight. This operator $M_a$ has special states called eigenvectors. You can think of an eigenvector as a state that $M_a$ doesn't really change. It just picks up a phase factor (a complex number of magnitude 1). The phase factors are $e^{2\pi i j/r}$ for $j = 0, 1, \ldots, r-1$. Notice that the period $r$ appears directly in these phase factors. If we could measure one of these phases, we could read off $r$.

That's exactly what the quantum phase estimation circuit does: it measures the phase of an eigenvector. But there's a catch. To prepare an eigenvector of $M_a$, you'd need to already know $r$, which is the thing we're trying to find.

The elegant workaround: prepare the state $|1\rangle$ instead. It turns out that $|1\rangle$ is the uniform superposition of all the eigenvectors:

$$|1\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}} \sum_{k=0}^{r-1} |\psi_k\rangle$$

So the circuit will measure the phase of a random eigenvector, giving us $k/r$ for some random $k$. From the denominator, we get $r$.

From measurement to period: the continued fractions table

In practice, the circuit doesn't measure the phase $k/r$ exactly. It uses $m$ qubits for precision, giving a register of size $Q = 2^m$. The measurement returns an integer $y$ that approximates $k \cdot Q / r$. To recover $r$, we divide $y$ by $Q$ and find the fraction with the smallest denominator that's close to $y/Q$. This is the continued fractions algorithm, a classical technique for finding the "simplest" fraction near a given decimal.

For $N = 15$ with 8 measurement qubits, $Q = 2^8 = 256$. Here is the full table of possible outcomes for $a = 2$, $r = 4$:

From period to factors: completing the algorithm

Once we know $r = 4$, the classical post-processing computes $a^{r/2} = 2^2 = 4$ and extracts $\gcd(4-1, 15) = 3$ and $\gcd(4+1, 15) = 5$. This works for all valid choices of $a$.

Building the actual circuit for $N = 15$

Now we're going to leave the abstractions behind and build the actual quantum circuit that factors $N = 15$. This is the same circuit that IBM and Google have run on their real quantum hardware. We'll work through it gate by gate, and at each stage we'll see exactly what happens to the quantum state.

For this walkthrough, we choose $a = 2$ (the smallest valid base for $N = 15$). The valid bases for $N = 15$ are $\{2, 4, 7, 8, 11, 13, 14\}$, and we know from our earlier exploration that $a = 2$ has period $r = 4$.

Choosing the register sizes

Two registers are needed:

The work register needs enough qubits to hold any value from $0$ to $N - 1 = 14$. That requires $n = \lceil \log_2 N \rceil = 4$ qubits, giving us states $|0\rangle$ through $|15\rangle$ (we just won't use $|15\rangle$).

The counting register needs enough qubits to give us a precise enough phase estimate. The standard choice is $m = 2n = 8$ qubits, giving us $Q = 2^m = 256$ possible measurement outcomes.3 With this precision, the continued fractions algorithm can reliably extract $r$ as long as $r < N$.

Total: 12 qubits (8 counting + 4 work).

The modular exponentiation operators $M_{2^{2^k}}$

The phase estimation circuit doesn't just need $M_2$. It needs $M_2$, $M_2^2 = M_4$, $M_2^4 = M_{16 \bmod 15} = M_1$, $M_2^8 = M_1$, and so on, controlled by each of the 8 counting qubits. Let's compute these powers:

$$2^{2^0} \bmod 15 = 2,\quad 2^{2^1} \bmod 15 = 4,\quad 2^{2^2} \bmod 15 = 16 \bmod 15 = 1$$

From $k = 2$ onward, the operator is just $M_1$, which is the identity (it does nothing). So we only need to actually implement two non-trivial operators: $M_2$ and $M_4$. The remaining six counting qubits don't need any controlled operation at all.

For $M_4$, the action is $M_4|x\rangle = |4x \bmod 15\rangle$, which corresponds to a different bit permutation:

$$|q_3 q_2 q_1 q_0\rangle \mapsto |q_1 q_0 q_3 q_2\rangle$$

This requires only two SWAP gates: swap $q_0 \leftrightarrow q_2$ and swap $q_1 \leftrightarrow q_3$.

Inside the inverse QFT

We've been treating the inverse QFT as a single box. But it's not really a single gate — it's a sequence of about $\frac{m(m+1)}{2}$ smaller gates. For our 8-qubit counting register, that's roughly 36 gates. Here is what's inside the box, shown for a simpler 4-qubit version (the structure scales the same way to 8 qubits):

Inverse QFT decomposition (4-qubit example)
Click an element type to explore
Three categories of gate make up the inverse QFT: bit-reversal SWAPs at the start, Hadamards on each qubit, and controlled phase rotations $R_k^\dagger$ between them.

Each of the small purple gates marked $R_k^\dagger$ is a controlled phase rotation. It applies a phase of $e^{-2\pi i / 2^k}$ to the target qubit, but only when the control is $|1\rangle$. The cascading pattern of these phase rotations is what implements the Fourier transform: it spreads the right phase information across the qubits so that interference reveals frequencies.

You don't need to memorize the QFT structure. The takeaway is: the QFT box in our main circuit is built from elementary single-qubit and two-qubit gates, the same kinds of gates that real hardware can natively execute. Nothing magical is happening inside, just careful interference engineering.

Going down to hardware gates

Real quantum hardware can only execute a small set of native gates. On IBM's superconducting processors, that set is roughly: arbitrary single-qubit rotations, plus a single two-qubit entangling gate (CNOT or CZ). Everything else has to be compiled into sequences of these.

The Toffoli gate is itself non-native on most superconducting hardware. It compiles further into roughly 6 CNOTs plus a handful of single-qubit gates. So one Fredkin becomes about 8 CNOTs total. Three Fredkins (one controlled-$M_2$) becomes about 24 CNOTs.

This is why circuit depth matters so much. A diagram that looks like a few clean boxes at the algorithmic level expands into hundreds of two-qubit gates at the hardware level. IBM reports a transpiled two-qubit gate depth of about 187 for the full $N=15$ circuit, and that's after extensive optimization.3

Tracing the state evolution

Now let's trace what happens to the quantum state at each stage of this circuit. This is the kind of analysis you can only really do on a simulator, since on real hardware measurement collapses everything. But it's exactly what makes the algorithm intuitive.

Stage 1: After the X gate on $t_0$. The work register is now in state $|1\rangle = |0001\rangle$. The counting register is still all zeros. The full state is:

$$|0\rangle_{\text{count}} \otimes |1\rangle_{\text{work}}$$

Stage 2: After all 8 Hadamards. The counting register is now in a uniform superposition of all $Q = 256$ possible values:

$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{256}} \sum_{x=0}^{255} |x\rangle_{\text{count}} \otimes |1\rangle_{\text{work}}$$

Stage 3: After the controlled modular exponentiation. Each value of $x$ in the counting register gets paired with $2^x \bmod 15$ in the work register. Because of the periodicity, the work register only ever holds one of four values: $\{1, 2, 4, 8\}$. The state becomes entangled:

$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{256}} \sum_{x=0}^{255} |x\rangle_{\text{count}} \otimes |2^x \bmod 15\rangle_{\text{work}}$$

Stage 4: After the inverse QFT on the counting register. The QFT detects the periodicity. Almost all of the amplitude piles up on values of the counting register that are multiples of $Q/r = 256/4 = 64$. So the counting register is concentrated on $\{0, 64, 128, 192\}$, each with probability about $1/4$.

Stage 5: Measurement. Reading out the counting register gives one of those four values, chosen at random. In binary (8 bits), they are:

Two of the four possible outcomes ($y = 64$ and $y = 192$) directly give us $r = 4$. The outcome $y = 128$ gives a divisor of the true period. The outcome $y = 0$ gives no information. So with about 50% probability per shot, a single measurement reveals the period. A few independent shots make success near-certain.

Completing the algorithm

Once we have $r = 4$, the rest is classical arithmetic. We compute $a^{r/2} = 2^2 = 4$, then:

$$\gcd(4 - 1, 15) = \gcd(3, 15) = \textcolor{#7c3aed}{\mathbf{3}}$$ $$\gcd(4 + 1, 15) = \gcd(5, 15) = \textcolor{#7c3aed}{\mathbf{5}}$$

And we have factored 15 = 3 × 5. The quantum circuit did the hard part (finding the period); the classical computer did the easy part (extracting the factors).

What this means for RSA encryption

RSA encryption, used to secure most of the internet, works like this: Alice picks two large primes $p$ and $q$, computes $N = p \times q$, and publishes $N$ as her public key. Anyone can encrypt a message using $N$, but only Alice (who knows $p$ and $q$) can decrypt it. The security of RSA rests entirely on the assumption that nobody can factor $N$ back into $p$ and $q$.

A typical RSA key uses $N$ with 2048 bits (about 617 decimal digits). The best classical factoring algorithms would take roughly $10^{23}$ years to crack such a key. Shor's algorithm reduces this to a matter of hours on a sufficiently powerful quantum computer.

This doesn't mean RSA is broken today. Current quantum computers have at most a few thousand noisy qubits. Factoring a 2048-bit number with Shor's algorithm would require an estimated 20 million physical qubits with error correction.4 But the threat is taken seriously enough that organizations like NIST have already standardized post-quantum cryptographic algorithms designed to resist quantum attacks.5 The transition to quantum-safe cryptography is underway.

The speed difference

The best classical factoring algorithm (the general number field sieve) runs in sub-exponential time. Shor's runs in polynomial time: $O(n^3)$ for an $n$-bit number.2 For a 2048-bit RSA key, that is the difference between roughly $10^{23}$ years classically and a few hours on a sufficiently large quantum computer.

Real qubits have errors

Everything we've shown so far assumes perfect qubits. The simulator uses ideal quantum operations: every Hadamard gate is exactly a Hadamard, every controlled-$M_2$ does exactly what it should, and every measurement returns a result drawn from the true probability distribution. Under those conditions, the circuit factors 15 reliably.

Real qubits don't behave that way. Today's quantum hardware suffers from several kinds of errors:

Decoherence. A qubit holds quantum information for only a brief moment before unwanted interactions with its environment scramble its state. On current hardware, this happens within microseconds. The longer your circuit takes to run, the more decoherence accumulates.

Gate errors. Each quantum gate is implemented by a physical pulse (microwave, laser, or otherwise) applied to the qubits. Pulses are never perfectly calibrated. Single-qubit gates have error rates around 0.01% on the best hardware, and two-qubit gates are roughly an order of magnitude worse. These errors compound: a circuit with thousands of gates accumulates substantial error.

Measurement errors. Even reading out the final state introduces noise. A qubit that should measure as $0$ will sometimes read out as $1$ and vice versa.

Recall our circuit for $N = 15$, $a = 2$. On an ideal simulator, only four bitstrings appear with equal 25% probability: $00000000$, $01000000$, $10000000$, and $11000000$. Run the same circuit on real IBM hardware and the result looks more like this:3

The four correct outcomes are still the four most common, so we can recover the answer with statistical filtering. But this only works because the circuit is small. For larger numbers, the circuit depth grows, more gates means more compounded error, and eventually the signal drowns in the noise. A 2048-bit RSA key would require a circuit so deep that with current error rates the answer would be completely undetectable.

Quantum error correction

The path to factoring real cryptographic numbers runs through quantum error correction. The idea is to encode each logical qubit (the kind we used above) into many physical qubits, in such a way that errors on the physical qubits can be detected and corrected without disturbing the logical state. This is how classical computers achieve essentially zero error rates: by using error-correcting codes on noisy underlying hardware.

Quantum error correction is harder than classical error correction. Quantum information can't be copied (the no-cloning theorem),6 and measurement collapses superposition. But schemes do exist (the surface code is the most studied) that can suppress errors arbitrarily, given enough physical qubits per logical qubit.7

The cost is enormous. Current estimates suggest that a single logical qubit reliable enough to run Shor's algorithm on a 2048-bit number would need about 1,000 physical qubits. The full algorithm needs about 20 million physical qubits running for around 8 hours.4 Today's largest quantum processors have a few thousand physical qubits with high enough error rates that error correction barely begins to work.

This is the great challenge facing quantum computing today. The algorithm is solved. The mathematics is understood. What remains is a hardware engineering problem of staggering difficulty: building a fault-tolerant quantum computer with millions of well-controlled qubits. Progress has been steady but slow. When (or if) we get there, the cryptographic infrastructure of the internet will have to be rebuilt around quantum-safe alternatives.

The full story of quantum factoring, then, has three acts. The mathematics (period-finding via the QFT) was settled by Shor in 1994. The circuit (the gates we built above) is well-understood and routinely simulated. The hardware, including the towers of error correction needed to make it work at scale, is what we're still building. We've shown you the first two acts. The third is being written right now in research labs around the world.

Looking back

We've seen that Shor's algorithm works through a chain of reductions. The problem of factoring a number is converted into the problem of finding the order of a modular exponential function. And order-finding is converted into quantum Fourier sampling, where superposition and interference do the heavy lifting.

Algorithm flowchart

There is a deeper lesson here that goes beyond the algorithm itself. For decades, computer scientists assumed that factoring was inherently hard. Not because anyone could prove it was inherently hard, but because nobody could find a fast algorithm, despite many smart people trying. Shor's algorithm showed that this difficulty was not a property of the problem. It was a property of the kind of computer being used to solve it.

Classical computers manipulate bits. Quantum computers manipulate qubits, which can exist in superposition and become entangled. These are not just engineering improvements over classical bits. They are fundamentally different physical resources, and they make certain computations tractable that were previously thought to be out of reach.

That said, it's important not to overstate the case. Shor's algorithm doesn't make quantum computers universally faster than classical ones. It exploits a very specific mathematical structure: the periodicity of modular exponentiation. Not all problems have this structure, and for many computational tasks, a classical computer remains the better tool. What Shor showed is that for the particular problem of factoring, the consequences of quantum mechanics are profound.
