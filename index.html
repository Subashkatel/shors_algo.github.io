Why factoring is hard

Every time you buy something online, send a private message, or log into your bank, your security depends on a single mathematical bet: that nobody can efficiently undo multiplication.

Multiplying two prime numbers is easy. Any computer can compute $61 \times 53 = 3{,}233$ in a fraction of a second. But going the other direction is surprisingly difficult. If I hand you the number $3{,}233$ and ask you to find its prime factors, you have to search. For this small number the search is quick. But for numbers with hundreds of digits, the kind used in real encryption, the search space is so vast that no known classical algorithm can finish before the sun burns out.

This asymmetry isn't some minor technicality. It's the foundation of RSA encryption, the system that protects most of the internet. The entire edifice of modern cryptography rests on the belief that factoring large numbers is effectively impossible.

In 1994, the mathematician Peter Shor showed that this belief has a crack. He discovered a quantum algorithm that factors numbers exponentially faster than any known classical method. What makes Shor's result so striking isn't brute force. The algorithm doesn't simply try factors faster. Instead, it transforms the factoring problem into something completely different: finding the period of a repeating mathematical function. And period-finding turns out to be something quantum computers do remarkably well.

The key idea: factoring reduces to period-finding

Here's the central insight, and it's worth spending some time to understand it well, because everything else in the algorithm flows from it.

Suppose you want to factor some number $N$. Pick any random number $a$ that shares no common factor with $N$ (in mathematical language, $\gcd(a, N) = 1$). Now look at what happens when you compute successive powers of $a$, taking the remainder after dividing by $N$ each time:

$$a^0 \bmod N,\quad a^1 \bmod N,\quad a^2 \bmod N,\quad a^3 \bmod N,\quad \ldots$$

If you haven't seen modular arithmetic before: $a \bmod N$ simply means the remainder when you divide $a$ by $N$. So $49 \bmod 15 = 4$, because $49 \div 15 = 3$ with remainder $4$.

The remarkable fact, guaranteed by number theory, is that this sequence always repeats. There is some smallest positive integer $r$, called the period or order, such that $a^r \equiv 1 \pmod{N}$, and then the entire pattern starts over from the beginning. Once you know $r$, and it happens to be even, the factors of $N$ can usually be found by a simple computation:

$$\gcd\!\left(a^{r/2} - 1,\; N\right) \qquad \text{and} \qquad \gcd\!\left(a^{r/2} + 1,\; N\right)$$

Why does this work? The reasoning is elegant. Since $a^r \equiv 1 \pmod{N}$, we know that $N$ divides $a^r - 1$. If $r$ is even, we can write $a^r - 1 = (a^{r/2} - 1)(a^{r/2} + 1)$. So $N$ divides this product. If neither factor is itself divisible by $N$, then $N$ must split its prime factors between the two terms. And $\gcd$ extracts them.

An analogy may help. Imagine a clock with an unknown number of hours. You watch the hand move, and after exactly $r$ ticks, it returns to where it started. That period reveals the hidden structure of the clock. Shor's algorithm uses the period of $a^x \bmod N$ to reveal the hidden structure of $N$ in exactly this way.

Worked example: factoring $N = 15$

Step 1: Choose a base

We pick $a = 7$. Verify: $\gcd(7, 15) = 1$. They are coprime, so we can proceed. (If the gcd were anything other than 1, we would have already found a factor without needing the quantum part at all.)

Step 3: Extract the factors

Since $r = 4$ is even, compute $a^{r/2} = 7^2 = 49$ and apply the gcd formula:

$$15 = 3 \times 5$$

Explore different choices of $a$

We chose $a = 7$ and everything worked out. But the algorithm says to pick $a$ at random. What happens with other choices? It turns out that different values of $a$ produce different periods. Some lead directly to the factors; others give odd periods or trivial results, and you simply try again with a new $a$. The probability of success on each attempt is at least $1/2$, so you rarely need more than a few tries.

Where the quantum computer comes in

At this point you might be wondering: we just found the period by computing a table. Why on earth do we need a quantum computer?

The answer is scale. For $N = 15$, the period is $4$, and we only needed to evaluate four values to find it. But for a number with 600 digits, the period could be comparably enormous. A classical computer would need to evaluate a staggering number of modular exponentiations, one after another, to find where the pattern repeats. No classical shortcut is known.

A quantum computer approaches this problem in a fundamentally different way. Rather than computing $a^x \bmod N$ for each value of $x$ one at a time, it places all possible values of $x$ into superposition and evaluates the function for all of them at once. Then it applies the Quantum Fourier Transform, which extracts the period from the interference pattern that results.

Deeper look: phase estimation and the multiplication operator

If you want to understand not just that the quantum circuit works, but why it works, the key is to see Shor's algorithm as an instance of a more general protocol called quantum phase estimation.

Consider the operation "multiply by $a$, modulo $N$." We can define this as a quantum operator $M_a$ that acts on basis states as $M_a|x\rangle = |ax \bmod N\rangle$. This operator is unitary: it simply shuffles the basis states around. For $N = 15$ and $a = 2$, it maps $|1\rangle \to |2\rangle \to |4\rangle \to |8\rangle \to |1\rangle$, cycling with period $r = 4$.

Now here is the critical observation. This operator $M_a$ has eigenvectors $|\psi_j\rangle$ with eigenvalues $e^{2\pi i j/r}$ for $j = 0, 1, \ldots, r-1$. If we could somehow prepare one of these eigenvectors and feed it into a phase estimation circuit, we would directly measure the phase $\theta = j/r$ and read off $r$ from the denominator.

But preparing the eigenvectors requires knowing $r$ in advance, which is the thing we are trying to find. The elegant solution: prepare the state $|1\rangle$ instead. It turns out that $|1\rangle$ is the uniform superposition of all the eigenvectors:

$$|1\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}} \sum_{k=0}^{r-1} |\psi_k\rangle$$

This means the phase estimation circuit, when given $|1\rangle$, will measure a random $k/r$ for some $k \in \{0, 1, \ldots, r-1\}$. From the denominator of this fraction, we recover $r$.

From measurement to period: the continued fractions table

In practice, the circuit uses $m$ qubits for measurement precision. For $N = 15$ with 8 measurement qubits, $Q = 2^8 = 256$. The measurement returns an integer that, divided by $Q$, approximates $k/r$. Here is the full table of possible outcomes for $a = 2$, $r = 4$:

Two out of four outcomes directly yield $r = 4$. The outcome $y = 128$ gives $r = 2$, which is a divisor of the true period. The outcome $y = 0$ is uninformative. With a few independent runs, the probability of determining $r$ correctly is very high.

From period to factors: completing the algorithm

Once we know $r = 4$, the classical post-processing computes $a^{r/2} = 2^2 = 4$ and extracts $\gcd(4-1, 15) = 3$ and $\gcd(4+1, 15) = 5$. This works for all valid choices of $a$.

What about real quantum hardware?

IBM has demonstrated Shor's algorithm on real quantum processors. For $N = 15$ with $a = 2$, the circuit requires 12 qubits (8 measurement + 4 target) and, after transpilation, has a two-qubit gate depth of about 187. Running on actual hardware introduces noise, but with error mitigation techniques, the correct factors can still be recovered. More recent demonstrations have factored $N = 21$ using compiled circuits on just 5 qubits.

The limiting factor for scaling Shor's algorithm to cryptographically relevant numbers (2048+ bits) isn't the algorithm itself but the hardware. Current estimates suggest that factoring a 2048-bit RSA key would require roughly 20 million noisy qubits and about 8 hours of computation.

What this means for RSA encryption

RSA encryption, used to secure most of the internet, works like this: Alice picks two large primes $p$ and $q$, computes $N = p \times q$, and publishes $N$ as her public key. Anyone can encrypt a message using $N$, but only Alice (who knows $p$ and $q$) can decrypt it. The security of RSA rests entirely on the assumption that nobody can factor $N$ back into $p$ and $q$.

A typical RSA key uses $N$ with 2048 bits (about 617 decimal digits). The best classical factoring algorithms would take roughly $10^{23}$ years to crack such a key. Shor's algorithm reduces this to a matter of hours on a sufficiently powerful quantum computer.

This doesn't mean RSA is broken today. Current quantum computers have at most a few thousand noisy qubits. Factoring a 2048-bit number with Shor's algorithm would require an estimated 20 million physical qubits with error correction. But the threat is taken seriously enough that organizations like NIST have already standardized post-quantum cryptographic algorithms designed to resist quantum attacks. The transition to quantum-safe cryptography is underway.

The speed difference

The best classical factoring algorithm (the general number field sieve) runs in sub-exponential time. Shor's runs in polynomial time: $O(n^3)$ for an $n$-bit number. For a 2048-bit RSA key, that is the difference between roughly $10^{23}$ years classically and a few hours on a sufficiently large quantum computer.

Looking back

We've seen that Shor's algorithm works through a chain of reductions. The problem of factoring a number is converted into the problem of finding the order of a modular exponential function. And order-finding is converted into quantum Fourier sampling, where superposition and interference do the heavy lifting.

Algorithm flowchart

There is a deeper lesson here that goes beyond the algorithm itself. For decades, computer scientists assumed that factoring was inherently hard. Not because anyone could prove it was inherently hard, but because nobody could find a fast algorithm, despite many smart people trying. Shor's algorithm showed that this difficulty was not a property of the problem. It was a property of the kind of computer being used to solve it.

Classical computers manipulate bits. Quantum computers manipulate qubits, which can exist in superposition and become entangled. These are not just engineering improvements over classical bits. They are fundamentally different physical resources, and they make certain computations tractable that were previously thought to be out of reach.

That said, it's important not to overstate the case. Shor's algorithm doesn't make quantum computers universally faster than classical ones. It exploits a very specific mathematical structure: the periodicity of modular exponentiation. Not all problems have this structure, and for many computational tasks, a classical computer remains the better tool. What Shor showed is that for the particular problem of factoring, the consequences of quantum mechanics are profound.
